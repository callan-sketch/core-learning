The Rotator Cuff

What it is, what it does, and why it matters for every shoulder movement you coach.

The four muscles

The rotator cuff is not a single muscle — it is a group of four muscles and their tendons that wrap the head of the humerus like a fitted cuff. Their tendons merge into a continuous sheet of tissue, distributing force across the whole joint rather than concentrating it at any single point.

A single integrated cuff

The four tendons don't attach separately — they merge into a continuous sheet of tissue that wraps the humeral head. This blending distributes forces evenly across the joint capsule, not concentrated at a single point.

Think of it as a fitted rubber cap stretched over a ball. Press anywhere on the cap and the whole thing resists — that's how the cuff distributes compressive force to hold the joint together.

Glide, roll & spin

Every shoulder movement involves three simultaneous motions at the joint surface. The rotator cuff's job is to control the ratio between them so the ball stays centred in the socket throughout the full range of motion.

What is roll?

Roll is like a ball rolling across a surface — one side of the humeral head contacts fresh areas of the glenoid as the arm lifts. Without correction, roll alone would push the ball off the top of the socket within just 20–30° of arm elevation.

The deltoid pulls in an almost vertical line — creating a large upward roll force with every contraction. Left unchecked, it would drive the ball straight into the acromion (the bony roof above the shoulder).

The cuff's response: all four cuff muscles pull the ball inferiorly and medially — compressing and depressing the head to counteract the upward roll force of the deltoid with every degree of arm elevation.

What is glide?

Glide is a pure sliding movement — the humeral head translates (slides) across the glenoid surface. To keep the ball centred, every roll in one direction must be paired with an equal and opposite glide.

When the arm rises, the ball rolls upward (deltoid force). The cuff simultaneously pulls the ball downward, producing an inferior glide that keeps the centre of the humeral head stationary relative to the socket. This is happening continuously and automatically.

This paired roll-and-glide is called arthrokinematics. The cuff makes it automatic throughout the full range of motion — you don't consciously command it, it happens with every movement.

What is spin?

Spin is pure rotation — the humeral head pivots on its own axis, with one point of the ball contacting the same point of the socket throughout. This occurs primarily during internal and external rotation movements.

Infraspinatus and teres minor are the primary external rotators — they spin the ball laterally. Subscapularis spins it medially. The ratio of spin to roll and glide is constantly adjusted by the cuff depending on arm position and load.

In practice all three motions occur together. The cuff's constant low-level activity blends them into smooth, damage-free movement across the full range of motion.

Stability vs power

The shoulder is the most mobile joint in the body — and mobility always trades against stability. The glenoid socket is notoriously shallow, covering only about 25–30% of the humeral head. The rotator cuff provides the dynamic stability that fills the gap.

The force couple — how big and small muscles cooperate

The big muscles create torque — rotational force that moves the arm through space. The rotator cuff creates compression — force directed straight into the socket. These two types of force are not in competition — they are complementary.

  • Deltoid — powerful abductor, creates upward shear on the humeral head
  • Latissimus dorsi — strong adductor and internal rotator, creates downward shear
  • Pectoralis major — powerful horizontal adductor, creates anterior shear
  • Rotator cuff — counteracts all shear forces by compressing the ball into the socket, acting as a dynamic fulcrum
The cuff does not move the arm. Its job is to hold the joint together while the lats, delts, and pecs do the moving — like a hand gripping a ball in place while the arm swings around it.

Why it matters

Understanding the cuff's role explains why it is the most commonly injured structure in the shoulder — and why the consequences of a weak or torn cuff go far beyond localised pain.

Impingement

A weak cuff cannot sufficiently depress the humeral head. During arm elevation the ball migrates superiorly and the supraspinatus tendon is pinched between the humeral head and the acromion. In most cases this reflects a cuff strength and control deficit rather than bony anatomy — and is addressable with targeted training.

Rotator cuff tears

Most tears begin as partial-thickness damage to the supraspinatus tendon from chronic impingement or overload. Over time partial tears become full-thickness. With a complete supraspinatus tear, the deltoid has no counterforce — the arm shrugs rather than abducts through a full arc.

Shoulder instability

A cuff that cannot maintain the compressive force couple allows the humeral head to translate excessively (subluxation). This is distinct from traumatic dislocation — it is chronic, often initially painless, and accelerates cartilage wear. Particularly common in overhead athletes.

Training implications

Antagonist balance is essential Climbers, swimmers, and overhead athletes chronically overdevelop the internal rotators (subscapularis, pec major, lat) relative to the external rotators (infraspinatus, teres minor). This imbalance loads the anterior capsule and accelerates cuff degeneration. A ratio of roughly 2:3 (internal:external rotation strength) is a commonly cited target.
Scapular positioning matters The cuff can only do its job if the scapula is correctly positioned — tilted posteriorly and upwardly rotated. A protracted, anteriorly tipped scapula narrows the subacromial space and alters every cuff muscle's line of pull. Lower trapezius and serratus anterior training addresses this and should precede or accompany rotator cuff work.
Train the cuff in its stabiliser role, not just in isolation Isolated external rotation exercises develop rotator strength, but the cuff also needs to be trained as a compressive stabiliser under load. Exercises like the bottoms-up kettlebell press, single-arm carries, and push-up plus progressions train the cuff to maintain joint centration during real movement — which is how it actually functions.
