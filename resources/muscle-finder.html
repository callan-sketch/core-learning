Shoulder & Chest

The shoulder complex is the most mobile region of the body. Large superficial muscles (deltoid, pectoralis major, latissimus dorsi) create movement while the rotator cuff provides dynamic stability. Scapular muscles (trapezius, serratus anterior) position the shoulder blade to optimise the line of pull of every muscle above.

Key imbalance

Most athletes overdevelop anterior (push) muscles relative to posterior (pull). Target a pulling:pushing ratio of at least 2:1 in volume.

Scapula first

No rotator cuff or deltoid exercise functions correctly if scapular stability is poor. Prioritise lower trapezius and serratus anterior before loading overhead movements.

Pec minor tightness

Tight pec minor tips the scapula anteriorly, narrows the subacromial space, and impairs overhead movement. A common contributor to shoulder impingement.

External rotation deficit

Insufficient infraspinatus and teres minor strength is the most common rotator cuff deficit in overhead athletes. Include face pulls and ER exercises every session.

Programming principleFor every pressing movement, include at least one pulling movement and one rotator-cuff-focused exercise. This ratio protects the anterior capsule, reduces impingement risk, and builds sustainable overhead strength.

Upper Arm

The anterior compartment (biceps, brachialis, coracobrachialis) primarily flexes the elbow; the posterior (triceps, anconeus) extends it. Balance between these groups is fundamental to elbow health and efficient pushing and pulling mechanics.

Brachialis is key

Most people train biceps but neglect the brachialis beneath it — the primary elbow flexor. Hammer curls and reverse curls are essential, not optional accessories.

Long head loading

Biceps long head and triceps long head both cross the shoulder. Shoulder position must be considered — incline curls and overhead extensions to fully load these heads.

Elbow valgus under load

During heavy curls the elbow often drifts inward (valgus), loading the medial collateral ligament and ulnar nerve. Cue neutral elbows throughout.

Tendinopathy

Both proximal and distal biceps tendinopathy respond well to progressive eccentric loading. Slow, controlled lowering phases are therapeutic.

Core & Trunk

The core includes deep stabilisers (transversus abdominis, multifidus), global movers (rectus abdominis, obliques, erector spinae), and accessory muscles (quadratus lumborum). Its primary role is force transfer between the upper and lower body, not aesthetics.

Deep core first

Transversus abdominis and multifidus should be activated before global movers are loaded. Without this, global muscles over-activate, creating excessive spinal compression.

Anti-movement > movement

The core's primary function is to resist unwanted movement, not produce it. Prioritise plank, side plank, Pallof press, dead bug.

IAP bracing

Intra-abdominal pressure — created by diaphragm, pelvic floor, and transversus abdominis — is the primary spinal protection mechanism under heavy load. Teach a 360° brace.

Posterior chain balance

Most core training over-emphasises the anterior at the expense of the posterior. For every trunk flexion exercise, include a corresponding posterior chain or anti-extension exercise.

The core is a force transfer centre, not an isolated muscle groupOn steep terrain or during heavy compound lifts, the core links upper and lower body force production. Train it functionally, under fatigue, and in the positions it will actually be used in.

Hip & Gluteal Region

The hip is the primary power transmission joint between the lower limb and the trunk. The gluteal muscles are among the largest in the body — yet chronically underactivated. Hip mobility directly limits squat depth, lunge mechanics, and high-step capacity.

Glute activation deficit

Prolonged sitting inhibits gluteus maximus via reciprocal inhibition. Always include a glute activation drill before heavy hip-dominant loading.

Hip flexor tightness

Tight iliopsoas anteriorly tilts the pelvis, compresses the lumbar spine, and limits hip extension — forcing the lumbar spine to compensate. One of the most common postural dysfunctions.

Gluteus medius — the stabiliser

Weak gluteus medius allows contralateral pelvic drop and ipsilateral knee valgus under load. A causative factor in patellofemoral pain, IT band syndrome, and hip impingement.

Adductor loading matters

Copenhagen adduction exercises dramatically reduce groin injury incidence. Include them proactively in any programme involving lateral or rotational lower body demands.

Quad:hamstring ratio

A hamstring-to-quadriceps ratio below 0.6 is associated with significantly elevated ACL injury risk. Hamstring strengthening — particularly eccentrically — should be prioritised.

VMO and patellar tracking

Vastus medialis oblique (VMO) weakness contributes to lateral patellar tracking and patellofemoral pain. Terminal knee extensions and step-downs specifically target VMO.

Hamstring injury mechanism

Most hamstring strains occur during high-speed eccentric loading. Nordic curls are among the most evidence-based injury prevention tools for this specific mechanism.

Rectus femoris vs vasti

Rectus femoris is the only quad that crosses the hip. When tight it limits hip extension in lunges. Stretch specifically by combining knee flexion with hip extension.

Lower Leg & Ankle

The lower leg controls ankle movement and foot position across four compartments. Achilles tendon loading reaches up to 8× bodyweight during running — the highest of any structure in the body. Calf and ankle strength are frequently undertrained relative to hip and quad work.

Achilles tendon loading

The Achilles experiences forces up to 8× bodyweight during running. It responds to progressive load — not rest. Eccentric heel drops and heavy slow resistance calf raises are the primary interventions for Achilles tendinopathy.

Ankle dorsiflexion range

Restricted ankle dorsiflexion (<35–38°) limits squat depth, increases knee valgus under load, and elevates plantar fascia stress.

Lateral ankle sprains

Peroneus brevis is the primary dynamic stabiliser against inversion. After a lateral sprain both proprioception and peroneal strength are compromised — both must be specifically rehabilitated.

The soleus gap

Most athletes train and stretch the gastrocnemius but neglect the soleus. Include seated calf raises and bent-knee calf stretches separately from their straight-leg equivalents.

The soleus is often the missing linkThe soleus is the more postural of the two calves and the primary load-bearer at low speeds. Ensure it is trained and stretched independently of gastrocnemius work.
